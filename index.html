Forget Tinkerbell: The Real "Good People" of Newfoundland

▾

If you came to Newfoundland expecting sparkling wings and granted wishes, you are in the wrong place. Folklore on the Rock is rugged, unpredictable, and often dangerous.

Fairy stories are a massive part of the province's oral history. They are just as culturally important to Newfoundland and Labrador as any physical artifact or historic building. You just need to understand one thing before you step out into the fog. These are not your everyday fairies.

The Origins of the "Little People"

The province's rich fairy folklore goes back to the Scottish, Irish, and English immigrants who brought their stories across the Atlantic in the 1600s.

Locals rarely call them "fairies" because doing so might draw their attention or offend them. Instead, people cautiously refer to them as the "Good People" or the "Little People." They definitely do not have gossamer wings. They come in all sizes and have been spotted in the shape of children, adults, glowing lights, and even animals. Sometimes they look exactly like us, only slightly off.

Sightings are not just relics of the distant past either. They are anchored by the real, lived experiences of Newfoundlanders today.

"The little fairies I saw were about, I would say, the size of two-year-olds, that height. When I came down the way, I saw a red bandana and it blew across the road. When I looked to see where it went, I saw two little fairies by a tree with bright red windbreakers and little peaked caps." — Mary Flynn, 97, of Otterbury, N.L.

Mischief, Malice, and the "Fairy Blast"

If you encounter the Good People, your best move is to walk the other way. They are notoriously mischievous and territorial. They are highly apt to entrance you, cause trouble, and lead you astray. Locals call this phenomenon being "Fairy-Led."

Outsiders are often surprised to learn that most Newfoundland fairies are troublemakers. Folklorist Barbara Rieti completed her PhD thesis on the subject at Memorial University of Newfoundland and later authored the book Strange Terrain: The Fairy World in Newfoundland. She explains the darker side of these encounters:

"The majority of fairies are not good fairies. They play tricks and lead you over the edge or a cliff. They'll change people. Or you'll get a fairy blast when they hit you, and then nasty stuff comes out of the wound, like sticks, balls of wool and fish bones." — Barbara Rieti, Folklorist

How to Protect Yourself on the Hunt

As you use this app to explore St. John's, you will be walking the exact margins where the Good People hunt. They love the edges of the woods, quiet alleys at dusk, and deep pockets of Rain, Drizzle, and Fog (RDF).

If you suddenly feel confused about where you are, or if you hear intoxicating accordion music coming from the empty barrens, you are probably being fairy-led.

To protect yourself, remember the old Bayman rules:

🍞
Carry Bread: Always keep a piece of hard tack or a crust of bread in your pocket. Fairies will not touch you if you carry the staple of life.
🧥
Turn Your Clothes Inside Out: If you are wandering in circles, take off your coat or sweater, turn it inside out, and put it back on. This breaks their spell.
🤫
Mind Your Manners: Never whistle in the woods at night, and always speak of them with respect.

Good luck out there. Keep your eyes on the fog, and hold onto your bread.

Privacy Policy

This app collects no personal data. We do not require registration, accounts, or login. All app data (your caught fairies, dismissed popups, and preferences) is stored locally on your device using your browser's localStorage. This data never leaves your device and is not transmitted to any server.

Location data: The Fairy Hunt feature uses your device's GPS with your explicit permission. Your coordinates are processed locally to calculate proximity to fairy hotspots and are never stored, logged, or transmitted to any server.

Weather data: We fetch weather conditions from the Open-Meteo API using the public coordinates for St. John's, NL. No personal data is sent in this request.

Google Sheets: Venue, lore, and game data is loaded from publicly published Google Sheets CSV files. No cookies or tracking are involved in these requests.

Third-party services: This app does not use analytics, advertising, cookies, tracking pixels, or any third-party data collection services.
